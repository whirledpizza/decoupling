Decoupling GDP Growth from Carbon Emissions Growth

Of 96 countries studied, 35 achieved absolute decoupling — GDP per capita rose while CO₂ per capita fell. These are concentrated in Europe, particularly post-Soviet economies that dismantled heavy industry in the 1990s, and Western European nations that shifted toward services and cleaner energy. A further 42 countries achieved relative decoupling, growing GDP per capita faster than CO₂ per capita on an annualised basis — meaningful progress, but CO₂ is still rising. The 16 countries with no decoupling are almost entirely in developing Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, where rapid industrialization drove emissions growth faster than income gains — a development story as much as a climate one.

Notes

Carbon productivity
GDP divided by consumption-based CO2 emissions (dollars of output per tonne of CO2). Higher means more economic output per unit of carbon.
GDP
Gross Domestic Product measured in real 2011 international dollars, adjusted for inflation. "Real" means price changes over time are stripped out so growth reflects actual output, not just rising prices.
PPP
Purchasing Power Parity adjustment makes GDP comparable across countries by accounting for differences in local prices. A dollar goes further in a low-cost country; PPP corrects for this so we compare actual living standards.
Consumption-based CO2
CO2 attributed to what a country consumes, including emissions embedded in imports, minus those embedded in exports. This differs from production-based (territorial) CO2, which counts only what is emitted within a country's borders. Rich countries that import manufactured goods often look better on production-based measures.
Per capita
All figures are divided by population. A country can show total GDP growth simply because its population grew; per capita figures reveal whether individuals are actually better off.
3-year averaging
Rather than comparing single years, all metrics use 3-year averages at each endpoint: 1990–92 for the start and 2021–23 for the end. This avoids both single-year noise in trade-based CO₂ estimates and the anomalous 2020 COVID year. GDP figures for 2021 and 2022 come from the World Bank (via OWID). The 2023 GDP per capita estimate is derived by applying IMF World Economic Outlook (April 2025) real GDP growth rates to the 2022 World Bank constant-dollar figure, adjusted for population growth. For 4 countries where IMF growth rates were unavailable (Czechia, Iran, South Korea, Turkey), the 2022 value is used as a proxy for 2023. CO₂ data for 2023 is available directly from OWID. On the right-hand trajectory chart, individual annual data points are still shown, but the reference lines (A and B) are anchored at the 1990–92 averaged values for consistency.
Excluded countries
Five countries are excluded for missing data: Norway and Namibia have no consumption-based CO₂ data for 1990; Brunei lacks GDP data; Panama has unreliable consumption CO₂ figures; and Malawi has a 2018 data discontinuity that appears to be a revision rather than a real change. A further nineteen countries are excluded due to unreliable trade-based CO₂ estimates: Bangladesh, Belgium, Botswana, Cambodia, Dominican Republic, Ethiopia, Guatemala, Guinea, Honduras, Kyrgyzstan, Malta, Mauritius, Nepal, Rwanda, Singapore, South Africa, Sri Lanka, Taiwan, and Togo. These show persistent oscillations and/or permanent step changes that corrupt the endpoint comparison — including cases where the trade CO₂ methodology was introduced mid-series (Belgium, South Africa, Cambodia, Sri Lanka, Malta, Mauritius), step changes within or just before the 2021–23 end window (Bangladesh, Ethiopia, Kyrgyzstan, Rwanda, Taiwan), and alternating spikes typical of estimation noise (the remainder). Year-by-year trajectories for all excluded countries remain viewable by clicking their names below the left chart.
